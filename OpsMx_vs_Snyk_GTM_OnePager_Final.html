The gap

What Snyk alone can't answer

"Is this specific release, right now, actually compliant and safe to ship?" Snyk has no visibility into the deployment event itself — OpsMx does.

Four things Snyk's architecture cannot do

Not because Snyk is weak — because deployment governance isn't the category it's built for.

01

Deployment Firewall

Don't stop at 'what is shipped'. Analyze 'where it is shipped'. Real-time, rule-based gate that blocks a release at the moment of deployment — not just at code review. Nothing passes into production without clearing it.

02

DBOM — proof of what shipped

An SBOM tells you what's in the code. A Delivery Bill of Materials tells you what actually got deployed, where, and how. Snyk can't answer that question — it isn't in the deployment path.

03

Compliance that enforces itself

NIST 800-53, FedRAMP, PCI DSS, and HIPAA packs are enforced automatically before release — not audited after the fact.

04

Works with the tools already in place

Still have a valid Snyk License? Bring you own Scanner and Correlate findings from Snyk, Trivy, Semgrep, and other scanners into one deployment risk decision as a unified platform.

Where Snyk wins — and why that's fine

Snyk's IDE plugins and developer-first workflow are genuinely best-in-class — that's not a talking point to argue around. Most enterprises we talk to already have Snyk, or something like it, and aren't looking to replace it. The conversation that actually lands isn't "switch away from Snyk," it's "your developers are covered — is your production environment?" OpsMx is built to sit downstream of exactly this kind of tool, not compete with it.
