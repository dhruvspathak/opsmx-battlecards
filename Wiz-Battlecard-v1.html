The gap

What Wiz alone can't answer

"Is this specific release, right now, actually compliant and safe to ship?" Wiz's graph tells you what's risky. It has no dedicated control plane that decides and proves whether a release is authorized to go out.

Four things Wiz's architecture wasn't built to do

Not because Wiz is weak its cloud security graph is excellent at what it does. Release governance simply isn't the category it was built for.

01

Correlate every engine you already run

Already running Wiz, Semgrep, Trivy, or another scanner for code review? OpsMx unifies SAST, SCA, container, and secrets findings from all of them including Wiz's own into one deployment risk decision. No forced migration onto a single vendor's engines.

02

A release gate that isn't Kubernetes-only

Wiz's Admission Controller blocks risky Kubernetes deployments. OpsMx's Deployment Firewall governs every release target Kubernetes, VMs, serverless, on-prem with the same policy-as-code gate, every time.

03

Exploitability with a decision attached

Wiz's Security Graph is genuinely strong at showing what's exploitable across code and cloud. OpsMx takes that same kind of signal plus KEV, EPSS, and business context one step further into an automated ship/no-ship decision, backed by a DBOM that proves what actually shipped, where, and when.

04

Compliance that enforces itself — independent of any one cloud

NIST 800-53, PCI DSS, and HIPAA policy packs are enforced automatically before release, not audited after the fact. And since Wiz became part of Google Cloud in March 2026, it's a fair question for procurement: OpsMx's governance layer isn't tied to any single hyperscaler's roadmap or pricing.

Where Wiz wins — and why that's fine

Wiz's cloud security graph is best-in-class — agentless CSPM, CIEM, and DSPM at a scale few platforms match, and Wiz for Government holds a genuine FedRAMP High authorization for the platform itself. That's not a talking point to argue around. Most enterprises we talk to already have Wiz, or are actively evaluating it, for exactly that reason, and aren't looking to replace it.

The conversation that actually lands isn't "switch away from Wiz," it's "your cloud posture is covered — is your release decision?" OpsMx is built to sit alongside exactly this kind of platform, correlating its findings into the deployment-governance layer it doesn't provide, not competing with its cloud coverage.
